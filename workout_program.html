What is a Split?

A split refers to how you break up your workouts over a week to allow your body to recover between exercise sessions and maximize your results, in other words you focus on different muscle groups on different days throughout the week. Split training allows more direct attention to each muscle group, improving muscle definition. It’s important to note that there is no best ultimate workout split. Your training split depends on your goals, your training age, your schedule, your experience and how much time you want to be spending in the gym.

What split suits you?

For example, when choosing a split as a gym newbie with tight schedule, a full-body program might be the ideal choice since you’re exercising your whole body with heavy compound movements (bench, deadlift, squats, rows) in one workout 3-4 times a week. It’s also a great way to get familiar with all essential gym lifts and equipment.

Once you’re an intermediate lifter, you might want to move to upper-lower split since your muscles now require more stimulus to grow with some isolation exercises on top. Unlike full-body split, here you’ll incorporate more specific exercises for each muscle group to provide better stimulus for growth as well as recovery.

If you’re an advanced lifter with years of experience behind, you might want to use push-pull-legs split. This program is usually adopted by many pro bodybuilders and fitness gurus, and it’s the one we’re going to be focusing a little more on. In PPL you hit all of the interconnected muscle groups at the same time/same workout twice a week, allowing at least 48 hours for recovery in between sessions, which is the most optimal way to build muscle.

  • on push days you’re using pushing movements, which involves muscles like chest, shoulders and triceps
  • On pull days you use pulling movements, which involves your back and biceps.
  • On leg days you obviously train your legs (quads, hamstrings and calves) which is a push category if you think about it but it’s been given its separate category.

To further optimize your training, you can add A and B days, for example on push A you start off with heavy bench and pull A with heavy rows, Push B you start off with heavy dumbbell press and Pull B with lat pulldowns. Or you can simply incorporate different exercises for the same muscle group from time to time. Your program doesn’t need to look exactly like mine.

Key Points

  • Slow on the eccentric and explosive on the concentric.
  • Control your form.
  • Figure out your muscular failure and train within 1-2 RIR on every set.
  • Log your progress and progressive overload.
