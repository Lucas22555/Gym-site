What is Hypotrophy?

Hypertrophy training is a style of training that focuses on building muscle mass and strength, but this time primarily focus is muscle mass. It’s about reaching that aesthetic “Greek God” look with artistic qualities like flow and proportion. If you’re a somewhat experienced lifter or simply did research on building muscle, you might’ve heard of hypertrophy or hypertrophy training approach.

what is muscular hypertrophy?

Muscular hypertrophy refers to an increase and growth of muscle mass. Muscle hypertrophy occurs in response to various stimulus such as strength, weight- and resistance training. This is why this word plays a huge role in the weightlifting industry.

How do muscles grow?

When our body is put under a significant amount of mechanical stress, stimulation occurs during contraction of the muscle. A repeated and systematic contraction (exercise) causes damage to the muscle cells. After the workout, while muscles are resting, our body repairs those damages, new muscles fibres replace the old, damaged ones, and this is where the muscle building process takes place. To speed up and optimize that process we need nutrition, or protein to be exact as a fuel. That’s why it’s crucial to take rest and refuel through nutrition, otherwise the breakup process will be greater than the recovery itself, which will take a hit on your overall well-being and progress.

What is the most optimal way to train for hypertrophy?

If your goal is to gain muscle, the most effective way to induce muscle hypertrophy out of many exercise variants is through weight training. In weight training there are several styles of gym training approaches, each with its own focus and benefits. It usually comes down to the 2 most common training styles – strength, hypertrophy. Since our primary focus is optimizing muscle mass, hypertrophy approach is the one to go, however first we should differentiate these styles to better know what we are working with. Strength training is a style of training that focuses on building strength and muscle mass, but primarily focus is building strength.

The are 5 key variables to consider differentiating these styles:

  • Volume
  • Intensity
  • Rest
  • Exercise selection
  • Diet

Volume

For Strength: Low-volume training - meaning performing fewer sets and reps with heavier weights. Ideal set and rep range variates between 2-3 sets 2-6 reps.

For hypertrophy: You should perform higher volume about 3-5 sets per exercise between 6-12 reps with slightly lighter weights, however it doesn’t mean that you don’t train hard, you still generate fatigue and stress by reaching true muscular failure.

Intensity

For Strength: High/absolute training intensity. Here you’d usually train with weights very close to your single weight-rep capacity – about 80-90% of your 1 RM, often performing the 1RM (100%) as well.

For hypertrophy: Hypertrophy training often involves more reps with a lower level of intensity, about 70% to 80% of your single weight-rep capacity (1RM). Here you rarely if ever test your true 1 RM.

Rests

For Strength: Higher rest periods High level intensity generates enough stress and fatigue, that’s why your rest period should be between about 3-5 minutes if not more. Without sufficient rest, you won’t be able to complete the necessary intensity needed to gain strength.

For hypertrophy: Although most research suggests about 1-2 minutes of rest, you should take as much rest as you need to fully recover for your next set. When you train to true muscular failure, 1-2 minutes might not be enough to walk in for your next set, meaning you won’t push as much weight and reps as you could. So take as much rest as you need to recover for your next set.

Exercise selection

For Strength: Strength athletes include exercises that involve several muscle groups and joints in one action, these exercises are heavy compound movements like bench, squat and deadlift.

For hypertrophy: on top of heavy compound movements you should also add isolation exercises to provide better and additional stimulus for that muscle to grow. Those isolation exercises are usually ones that performed with lighter weight and higher reps.

Diet

For Strength: Strength athletes tend to be in a higher caloric surplus than a guy who trains for hypertrophy for example since it requires more mass to progressively lift more weight overtime.

For hypertrophy: Slight or moderate caloric surplus is recommended to gradually build mass to avoid having too much fat on top. But overall, there’s no big difference between diet of an strength athlete and a bodybuilder

It’s important to note that these styles a very close to each other and can work together. It’s not like by focusing primarily on hypertrophy you’ll completely neglect strength or the other way other around, but at the end of the day its within the specific training approach which will acquire your desired goal.

Concept of failure and progressive overload

Training to failure and progressive overload are two fundamental concepts in strength and hypertrophy training, each playing a crucial role in enhancing your training performance and optimising muscle growth.

Training to failure involves performing an exercise until it's physically impossible to complete another repetition with proper form. This approach aims to fully exhaust the targeted muscle fibres, stimulating maximal muscle recruitment, leading to effective muscle growth. By pushing muscles to their limits, training to failure is the most effective strategy for increasing both strength and muscle hypertrophy.

Summary

  • Muscular hypertrophy refers to an increase and growth of muscle mass.
  • Prioritise hypertrophy training approach, but you can also incorporate strength training routine and do both.
  • Train close to muscular failure
  • Progressive overload overtime and log (write down) your progress.
